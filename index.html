Emails That Feel
Modern

A design guide for the Rebel design team.
Scope Agency-wide
Applies To All Email Clients
Status Active

Modern ecom emails feel like a scroll, not a stack. The reader should move through the email the way they'd scroll a well-designed website — one continuous surface with natural rhythm, breathing room, and zero visual friction.

If an email looks like a series of rectangles placed on top of each other, it's not modern — regardless of how good the photography is. Structure is the thing that most designers get wrong, and it's the first thing a reader feels even if they can't name it.

This guide applies across all clients. When client aesthetics conflict with these principles, the client wins — but the structural rules always hold. For client-specific calibration, see each CLIENT.md.

Reference brands Vessel BREZ Apple One Sol
01
Principle 01
One Canvas, No Sections

The email reads as a single scrollable surface. There are no visible section breaks, no dividing lines, and no background color changes that create a hard edge between content areas. Think of it as one long canvas the reader scrolls down — not a series of content boxes stacked vertically.

Do
  • Run a single background color, gradient, or image treatment the full length of the email
  • Use gradient fades to transition between sections — colors bleed into each other
  • Let photography extend beyond its content boundary so it flows into the next section
  • Match background colors between sections so the seam is invisible
Don't
  • Alternate white and grey section blocks — the #1 signal of a template email
  • Add horizontal rules or dividers between sections
  • End a section with a hard rectangular crop or color stop
  • Use section-level background colors that contrast sharply with neighbors

02
Principle 02
Floating Logo — No Header Block

The brand mark sits directly on top of the hero image or background. No background container, no padding block, no colored strip holding the logo. It simply exists at the top of the email as if placed there. This is one of the clearest signals separating a modern email from a template.

Do
  • Place the logo centered, floating over the hero image or email background
  • Use a logo version (white or dark) that works directly on the email's background
  • Leave the logo as the only element in its vertical space — no nav links beside it
  • Keep the logo small and restrained — it identifies, not decorates
Don't
  • Create a separate "header" row with its own background color behind the logo
  • Put navigation links or social icons beside the logo in the header
  • Add a drop shadow to the logo to make it visible — pick a logo that works on the background
  • Use a different background for the header than the rest of the email

03
Principle 03
Photography That Melts, Not Crops

Images don't end at a rectangle. Hero shots, lifestyle photography, and product images fade out at their edges — typically at the bottom — dissolving into whatever section comes next. The image's boundary becomes invisible. This is the single most noticeable technique in modern email design and is hard to unsee once you know it.

Do
  • Apply a bottom-to-transparent gradient fade on hero and lifestyle images
  • Match the fade's end color exactly to the background of the next section
  • Let product photography extend beyond its "section" into the one below
  • Treat the fade as part of the design — it's not a fix, it's the technique
Don't
  • Drop images into hard-edged boxes — visible corners kill the effect immediately
  • Place a product shot on a contrasting background that reveals the image boundary
  • Use a rectangle mask where a fade belongs
  • Let the photo's native background color clash with the email's background

04
Principle 04
Content Structure: Header / Subheader / Repeat

This is the most important structural rule. Modern emails do not use product blocks, feature grids, or icon + description layouts as the primary content structure. They use visual hierarchy and vertical repetition. Each point gets a headline. The headline carries the weight. The subheader supports it. Then it repeats.

The problem with product blocks: When copy arrives with a list of features, the instinct is to build a 3-column icon grid or a table-style layout. Those layouts create visible "blocks" that look rigid and non-modern — even if every other design decision is right. The structure fights the aesthetic.
Exception — infographics: Timelines, comparison charts, and structured data visuals are not product blocks. When the content is genuinely visual (a journey, a side-by-side comparison, a breakdown), use an infographic format — but match it to the right style. See Principle 11 for the three approved formats.
[Large headline — the hook or claim]
[1–2 line supporting sentence]
[Optional: CTA button]

[Large headline — next point]
[1–2 line supporting sentence]
[Optional: CTA button]
Do
  • Convert feature lists into headline/subheader pairs stacked vertically
  • Let the typography carry the structure — no boxes needed
  • Use icons as small accent elements below a headline, not as the primary content carrier
  • Give each benefit its own visual moment — one per "section" in the flow
Don't
  • Build 3-column icon grids — this is the single most common modern-email mistake
  • Create "feature" sections with boxed cards around each feature
  • Use side-by-side icon + paragraph as the main content structure
  • Let the copy's bullet list shape drive the design into a table or grid

05
Principle 05
Pill CTAs — Never Square

Every call-to-action button uses a fully rounded pill shape — no sharp corners, no square edges. Button style communicates brand posture before the copy even lands. Square buttons signal old-school templates. Pill buttons signal modern, considered design. Width is proportional to content, never full-bleed.

Do
  • Outlined pill — transparent fill, colored border + text. Works on dark backgrounds. Premium, confident. (Vessel)
  • Filled pill — solid color fill. Works when stronger CTA contrast is needed. (BREZ lime-green, One Sol warm solid)
  • Pill with arrow — "Shop now →" style. Subtle, editorial, great for secondary CTAs. (BREZ)
  • Size the button to its copy + comfortable padding — never wider than ~55% of the email column
Don't
  • Use square or sharp-cornered rectangle buttons under any circumstance
  • Use full-width buttons that span the entire email width — they read as default template
  • Vary the button style within the same email (pick one style and hold it)
  • Use more than two distinct CTA styles in one email

Principle 06
Commit to One Color Palette

Each email owns one dominant color. Every section, background, and visual element should feel like it came from the same palette decision. If the email has an accent color (usually for CTAs), that's one accent — not multiple. An email that reads as patchwork is an email that wasn't designed.

Do
  • Choose the primary brand color or a seasonal palette at the brief stage — before building
  • Run that color or its close tones the full length of the email
  • Use one accent color for CTAs if needed — maximum two colors total
  • Treat neutral (black, white, off-white) as invisible — it doesn't count against the palette
Don't
  • Mix brand colors, seasonal tones, and photography backgrounds without a unifying decision
  • Let each section have its own color story — sections should feel like the same email
  • Default to brand guidelines mechanically — ask what palette fits the campaign angle
  • Use more than one accent color across the email

Principle 07
Space Is Design

White space — or dark space, or negative space — is not wasted space. It's what separates premium from cheap. The amount of breathing room around elements is one of the loudest signals about whether a brand takes itself seriously. Apple is the extreme reference. Vessel is close. The instinct to fill empty space is almost always wrong.

Do
  • Give headlines generous vertical padding — above and below
  • Add spacing between the image and the next headline that feels slightly too much — it probably isn't
  • Treat the space itself as part of the composition, not a gap to fill
  • When in doubt, add more padding, not more content
Don't
  • Stack content blocks with no breathing room between them
  • Fill blank areas with decorative elements — if it looks empty, the spacing might be right
  • Use tight padding inside CTA buttons — generous padding is part of the pill look
  • Reduce spacing to fit more content — cut the content instead

08
Principle 08
Typography Hierarchy

Headlines should be large enough to read at a scroll-stop glance. The size jump between headline and body copy should feel exaggerated — at minimum 2× the size. Body copy supports; it never leads. The typography itself should communicate the brand's confidence level before the reader reads a single word.

Do
  • Make headlines significantly larger than they feel necessary — bigger is almost always right
  • Use a clear two-level hierarchy: display/headline and body. Three levels max
  • Consider mixing a display serif (editorial, warm) with a clean sans-serif (body) — BREZ does this well
  • Use letter-spacing on uppercase display text — tight tracking reads as modern
Don't
  • Use the same size for headlines and body copy — no hierarchy = no design
  • Bold mid-paragraph body copy, add underlines, or color-highlight words in body text
  • Use more than two type weights in one email (e.g., thin + regular + bold = too many)
  • Set body copy larger than 16px — if you need to, the copy is too long

09
Principle 09
Technical Callouts (When Applicable)

When a product has specific technical features that warrant calling out, use annotation-style labels on the product image — not a bullet list or features table. This is the editorial technique used in magazine product spreads and Apple spec pages. It reads as premium, confident, and product-first.

Do
  • Place the product image large and let annotation labels point to specific details
  • Use short labels (2–5 words) — the image does the showing, the label names it
  • Keep annotation lines thin and minimal — this is a spec diagram, not a callout bubble
  • Apply this technique for performance/technical products where specs are a selling point
Don't
  • Pull features out of the image and list them in a box or table below the product shot
  • Use checkmarks or bullet points for product specs — it reads as a comparison, not editorial
  • Overcrowd the product image with too many callouts — 3–5 max
  • Apply this technique to lifestyle/brand-feel emails — it's for technical product moments only

Principle 10
Clean Footer

The footer should feel like it belongs to the email, not like a required legal block bolted on after the real design ended. A heavy, block-styled footer restarts the email visually and signals that the designer stopped caring after the last CTA. Keep it minimal. Match it to the email's color story.

Do
  • Match the footer background to the email's dominant color or let it fade to dark/neutral
  • Limit navigation links to 3–4 max, small text, horizontal layout
  • If press logos are on-brand (e.g., BREZ), use them as a minimal credibility strip
  • Keep legal/unsubscribe text very small and in the email's muted color — present but not loud
Don't
  • Create a heavy, color-blocked footer section that visually "restarts" the email
  • Load the footer with social icons, large imagery, or promotional content
  • Use a footer background that contrasts strongly with the email body
  • Add so many links that the footer reads as a navigation menu

Principle 11
Infographics — Three Approved Formats

When the brief calls for structured visual content — comparisons, timelines, data breakdowns — don't default to a generic table or bullet list. Use one of three formats, each matched to the type of content. The format choice should feel inevitable given the content: annotation for product specs, floating blocks for comparisons, timeline for sequential journeys.

Format 01
Annotation Callouts

Use for: Product specs, feature highlights, technical details tied to a physical product.

Labels point directly at the relevant part of the product in the image. Lines are thin and minimal. The product image does the showing — the label just names it. 3–5 callouts max. This is a magazine spec diagram, not a feature list.

Format 02
Floating Blocks

Use for: Product comparisons, variant breakdowns, ingredient lists, multi-item showcases.

Rounded cards with generous internal padding, sitting on the email background. They float — they don't snap to a hard grid. Cards have breathing room between them. Each card has a clear headline and a short supporting line. Never use more than 3 cards per row; single-column stacking is often cleaner.

Format 03
Journey / Timeline

Use for: Onboarding sequences, day-by-day breakdowns, step-by-step processes, before/after arcs.

A visual flow with numbered milestones, a connecting path or line, and short story-driven copy at each step. Should feel editorial and narrative — not a process diagram or flowchart. The copy at each step should read like a moment, not a task.

Do
  • Match the format to the content type — annotations for specs, floating blocks for comparisons, timeline for journeys
  • Keep cards rounded with generous padding — sharp corners and tight text make blocks look like spreadsheets
  • Write milestone copy in the timeline as moments, not instructions
  • Let infographics breathe — same spacing rules apply as to the rest of the email
Don't
  • Use an HTML-style table or a hard-edged comparison matrix — these look like a spreadsheet
  • Build a 3-column grid and call it a comparison — if the columns align to a rigid grid, it reads as a template
  • Mix formats in one email — pick one infographic style and commit to it
  • Use a generic flowchart or diagram style for timelines — it should feel like a story, not a process doc

Application
Client Calibration

Not every client gets the same intensity. Use the brand's website and existing assets as your calibration reference. When starting a new client email, ask: what is the emotional feeling of their website? The email should feel like it could live there. These rules tell you how to adjust the dials.

Brand Signal
Dial Up
Dial Down
Dark / moody brand
High contrast, dramatic photography fades, dark canvas throughout, near-black backgrounds
Avoid bright or warm tones — they fight the brand's identity
Bright / energetic brand
Bold gradients, warmer or saturated tones, more playful photography treatment
Reduce heaviness and formality — lightness is part of the brand
Feminine / lifestyle brand
Softer fades, more white space, gentler color transitions, editorial photo treatment
Less bold type, fewer high-contrast moments — warmth over impact
Technical / performance brand
Annotation callouts, spec-forward layouts, structured product diagrams, precise copy
Remove decorative or lifestyle-feel elements — function leads here
Luxury / premium brand
More white space, smaller and quieter CTAs, restrained palette, less copy
No bold/energetic elements — remove anything that feels promotional or loud

Pre-Send
Design Checklist

Reference this before submitting any email design. Every item should be true before the file goes out.

–
No hard-edged section breaks — the email reads as one scrollable canvas
–
Logo floats directly on the hero — no header block or background container
–
Photography fades at edges — no hard rectangular image crops visible
–
No 3-column icon grids or generic feature boxes — use header/subheader structure instead
–
If the brief has structured visual content, one of the three infographic formats has been applied (Principle 11)
–
CTA button shape matches or complements the client's brand — no default square corners
–
CTA buttons are proportionally sized — not full-width
–
One dominant color palette throughout — maximum one accent color
–
Generous spacing around headlines, images, and CTAs — elements breathe
–
Headline size is dramatically larger than body copy — minimum 2× difference
–
Maximum two typeface weights used in the email
–
Footer background matches or flows from the email's color story
–
Footer navigation is minimal — 3–4 links max
–
Design has been calibrated against the client's website aesthetic — it could live there
